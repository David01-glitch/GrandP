Crafts

10 Easy Crafts to Do With Grandkids

Simple, low-mess craft ideas that work for ages 3 through 12 — using things you already have at home.

1. Paper plate animals — paint, glue, googly eyes. A 30-minute hit.

2. Salt-dough handprints — flour, salt, water, bake low. Keep one for yourself.

3. Nature collages — collect leaves on a walk, glue into a scene.

4. Painted rocks — kindness rocks to hide in the neighborhood park.

5. Tissue paper suncatchers — contact paper + scraps of color = magic windows.

Tip: lay down a vinyl tablecloth before you start. Cleanup time is bonding time too — give them a sponge and a song.

Games

Games That Span Generations

From classic card games to backyard favorites, the right game closes the age gap fast.

Card games: Go Fish, Crazy Eights, and Memory work for ages 4+. Rummy and Hearts for ages 9+.

Board games: Ticket to Ride First Journey, Sushi Go, Uno — short rounds, low frustration.

Backyard: bocce, ring toss, and cornhole let everyone play sitting or standing.

Imagination games: "I Spy," 20 Questions, and storytelling chains need zero equipment.

Don't worry about winning — the goal is the laughs between turns.

Connection

Staying Close From Far Away

When grandkids live across the country, small rituals matter more than long visits.

Weekly video story time: read the same book together — send them a copy first.

Mailbox surprises: postcards, stickers, or a $1 toy in a padded envelope. Kids love physical mail.

Shared show: watch the same kids' show and text them about it the next day.

Voice memos: record bedtime stories or songs they can replay anytime.

Consistency beats grandness. A 10-minute weekly video call lands deeper than a once-a-year visit.

Gift Guide

Age-Appropriate Gift Guide

What to give (and what to skip) at every stage — without breaking the bank.

Ages 0–2: soft books, stacking cups, wooden teethers. Skip anything with small parts.

Ages 3–5: dress-up costumes, magnetic tiles, simple puzzles, picture books.

Ages 6–9: art supply kits, science kits, beginner board games, chapter books.

Ages 10–12: strategy games, building sets, journals, gift cards for hobbies they love.

Teens: ask. Seriously — a thoughtful note plus a gift card to their favorite store wins.

Experiences (zoo trip, baking afternoon, movie date) often beat objects. They remember the time, not the toy.

Emotional

Handling the Tough Moments

Tantrums, sadness, big feelings — how grandparents can be a steady presence.

Listen first. Don't fix, don't lecture. "That sounds really hard" goes a long way.

Validate, then redirect. "I'd be upset too. Want to take a walk together?"

Respect parent rules. Even if you'd do it differently, alignment matters.

Offer presence, not solutions. Sitting next to a sad child quietly is its own gift.

Your superpower as a grandparent is calm. You've seen big feelings come and go. That perspective is healing.

Recipes

Cooking Together: Easy Recipes

Three no-fail recipes that grandkids can mostly make themselves with a little help.

No-bake cookies: oats, peanut butter, honey, chocolate chips. Mix, scoop, chill.

Personal pizzas: tortillas + sauce + cheese + toppings. 8 minutes in the oven.

Fruit smoothies: frozen banana, berries, milk, a spoon of yogurt. Let them push the blender button.

Cooking teaches measuring, patience, and following directions — without feeling like a lesson.
